## Overview

The Trust Transfer Problem states that AI-generated artefacts can destroy the trust they are trying to build if a customer — reading critically — encounters a single moment of credibility failure. The auditor persona is not hostile; she *wants* this product to work. That makes the failures more damaging: they puncture motivated belief.

Three specific moments where trust collapses:

---

## Moment 1

**Quoted line:** *"MangoMind AI detects burnout 3 weeks before it manifests"*  
**File:** `MangoMind/index.html` (hero section headline, rendered as "Know your team is burning out 3 weeks before they do")

**Why trust collapses here:**  
The auditor persona has a CIPD qualification and has read the Maslach Burnout Inventory literature. She knows that burnout "manifestation" is not a discrete event — it is a process measured across dimensions (exhaustion, cynicism, efficacy) that clinicians track over weeks of validated instrument use. The claim that an AI can detect burnout onset at a precise 3-week horizon with sufficient reliability to be actionable, without a single clinical trial, peer-reviewed study, or dataset disclosure in the landing page, reads not as a bold value proposition but as hallucinated precision. The specific number ("3 weeks") signals either that the founders have clinical evidence they have not published, or that the number was generated by a pipeline and no one pushed back on it. An HR manager who has sat through a real burnout crisis knows the difference — and the absence of evidence transforms the headline from inspiring to alarming.

---

## Moment 2

**Quoted line:** *"94% Beta customer retention at 90 days"*  
**File:** `MangoMind/pitch.html` (Slide 8: Traction)

**Why trust collapses here:**  
The same slide states "5 beta customers actively using MangoMind." A 94% retention rate calculated on n=5 customers at 90 days — where 100% retention is 5 customers and 94% is geometrically impossible (you cannot have 4.7 retained customers) — is a statistic that does not survive arithmetic scrutiny. The auditor persona, evaluating the deck as a potential enterprise customer who has also reviewed SaaS tools before, will immediately flag this as either a fabricated metric or an innumerate application of a percentage to a sub-10 cohort. The moment she reaches for her calculator is the moment trust exits the room. Metrics in a pitch deck exist to build confidence; a metric that breaks under basic inspection destroys more confidence than no metric at all.

---

## Moment 3

**Quoted line:** *"Our system is fully GDPR-compliant and operates under a completed Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)."*  
**File:** `MangoMind/index.html` (Privacy & Compliance section)

**Why trust collapses here:**  
The auditor persona is purchasing a product that will be deployed to all 65 of her employees and will involve processing metadata about their workplace behaviour. Before signing any contract, her legal and compliance team will ask: "Can we see the DPIA summary?" The landing page asserts completion of a DPIA but provides no link, no summary, no DPC consultation reference, and no named Data Protection Officer. Under GDPR Article 13, the data subject (every employee) is entitled to this information before processing begins. For an HR manager who will need to present this to her employees and to her own legal counsel, the assertion of compliance without any verifiable evidence is worse than silence — it suggests the founders do not understand what a DPIA is for. The claim manufactures trust and then immediately withdraws the substance that would support it.
