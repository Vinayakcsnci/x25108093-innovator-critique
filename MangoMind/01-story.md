## The Story

Aoife Brennan had worked in HR at a mid-sized Dublin tech firm for eleven years. She had watched the mangoes.

Every summer, the office manager ordered a crate of Alphonso mangoes — a small extravagance from a vendor in the English Market in Cork who sourced them directly from India. The mangoes arrived firm, travel-stressed, their skins a dull ochre. Left alone, most went overripe before anyone noticed. But the ones that were checked daily — pressed gently, sniffed, rotated — were caught at peak ripeness. Almost no one noticed that Aoife always caught the right ones. She had learned to track the invisible signals.

Aoife's other obsession was dolphins. The Irish coast — particularly Fungie's old bay in Dingle — had made her deeply familiar with how bottlenose dolphins communicated stress. Marine biologists she'd volunteered with explained that a dolphin pod displays micro-behavioural signals — surfacing frequency changes, altered vocalisations, reduced synchronised swimming — weeks before an individual becomes visibly ill. The pod tracks these signals collectively. No single dolphin carries the full picture; the intelligence is distributed.

In the spring of 2025, Aoife lost three colleagues to burnout in six months. One was hospitalised. The others left quietly. In retrospect, she had seen the signs — the missed meetings, the clipped Slack replies, the gone-quiet energy. She had seen them but had no system to act on them.

"We track Net Promoter Score obsessively," she told her CEO. "We track sales pipeline, server uptime, customer churn. We track nothing about the humans who produce all of it. We wait for the mango to go soft before we check it."

That conversation became MangoMind.

MangoMind is an AI-powered employee wellbeing and burnout-tracking platform built for Irish small and medium-sized businesses. Like the dolphin pod that reads distributed signals, MangoMind aggregates anonymous micro-signals from employees — check-in sentiment, calendar load, after-hours messaging patterns — to build a real-time burnout risk index for teams. Like the mango check, it surfaces the fruit before it falls.

Aoife left her HR role in September 2025 and began building MangoMind in partnership with Dr. Ciarán Ó Murchú, a clinical psychologist at UCD, and Ronan Fahy, a former CTO at a Dublin fintech. Their shared belief: burnout is predictable, and in Ireland — where workplace mental health costs an estimated €11 billion per year — that prediction is worth building.
