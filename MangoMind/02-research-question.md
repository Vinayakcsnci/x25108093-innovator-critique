## Primary Research Question

**Can AI-driven, anonymised analysis of workplace behavioural micro-signals reliably predict employee burnout risk at the team level with sufficient lead time (≥2 weeks) to enable preventative HR intervention — and can such a system be deployed in Irish SMBs in a manner that is GDPR-compliant, ethically defensible, and commercially viable?**

---

## Sub-Questions

1. **Signal validity:** Which passive behavioural signals (messaging frequency, calendar density, after-hours activity, check-in sentiment) are statistically predictive of self-reported burnout as measured by validated instruments (MBI, CBI)?

2. **Anonymisation feasibility:** Can individual-level signals be sufficiently aggregated at team level to prevent re-identification, while retaining predictive power?

3. **Lead time:** What is the minimum lead time between detectable signal deviation and clinical burnout onset, and does this vary by industry, role level, or gender?

4. **Intervention efficacy:** Does an AI-surfaced early warning to an HR manager, without identifying individuals, reduce burnout incidence compared to a control group?

5. **Irish market context:** What are the specific regulatory constraints under Irish employment law and the EU AI Act that would govern the deployment of a system classifying employee wellbeing states?

6. **SMB adoption:** What barriers prevent Irish SMBs from adopting employee wellness technology, and what product characteristics would overcome those barriers?

---

## Framing Note

This question sits at the intersection of occupational psychology, privacy law, and applied machine learning. It is generative: answering it requires both empirical research (signal validity studies, pilot deployments) and normative analysis (what constitutes acceptable AI use in an employment context under Irish and EU law). The research question is therefore both a product design question and an ethics question — a distinction the MangoMind team treats as foundational.
